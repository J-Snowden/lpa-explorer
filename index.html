The people the dashboard cannot see

Standard reporting cuts a workforce by department, tenure, and level. Grouping people instead by how they answered adds a layer those cuts can't produce on their own — one that separates the worst experiences from the best far more sharply, because what drives the difference runs across the groups the org chart already knows about.

▸ The method behind this — why an assessment technique transfers to employee experience

A research team asked 1,698 middle- and high-school students not whether they passed a science test, but how it felt. The answers sorted into four kinds of experience — and the students having the worst time were not identifiable in advance from any demographic characteristic the team examined; the profiles cut clean across every group. That finding is what this page reconstructs, in an employee context, on synthetic data.

Assessment experience and employee experience are the same measurement problem. Both ask people how something felt; both need the instrument validated before the answers mean anything; and both are usually reported by sorting people into groups defined in advance — department, tenure, level. Latent profile analysis is a layer that runs alongside that reporting, grouping people by their answers rather than their attributes. That added layer is what this page is about.

The method here is real: a peer-reviewed study of 1,698 students, presented at AERA 2026, where I was a co-equal first author and contributed the factor analysis, the latent profile analysis, and the model selection.

Herrmann Abell, C. F., Deverel-Rico, C., Snowden, J., Brubaker, A., Campanella, M., Flanagan, J., Lee, D., Olson, P., & Wilson, C. D. (2026). Centering Student Voice in Science Assessment through Leveraging Student Experience Data. AERA Annual Meeting, Los Angeles.

The data on this page are synthetic — generated from stipulated parameters, because the real responses belong to the research organisation and were collected from minors under IRB protocols. Nothing here is a finding of the published study; it is a demonstration of its method, moved from an assessment setting into an employee-experience one.

The finding

The people the dashboard cannot see

Dashboards are built to summarise a workforce by its recorded attributes — department, tenure, level. A latent profile analysis adds what they aren't built to show: it groups respondents by the pattern of their answers instead. Applied here, it separates four kinds of experience.

The decision

How many kinds of experience?

The profiles are not given; the analyst chooses how many to keep. That choice is not cosmetic — it decides whether the worst-off group exists as something you can staff for. Change it and watch the recommendation change.

This choice is a judgement. The evidence behind it is in the model-selection section below.

The evidence behind the choice

Everything above rests on a model-selection decision and a measurement model. Both are here in full, for the reader who wants them.

▸ The model selection, in full — 18-model grid, four criteria, and what each does and does not settle

The measurement model was validated first

Before any profiles were extracted, the five-factor structure was confirmed on held-out data. The sample was split 50/50: an exploratory analysis on one half, a confirmatory fit on the other, so the structure is not discovered and confirmed on the same respondents. Scores were then extracted from the winning model refit on the full sample.

Four criteria, two of them silent

The retained solution is Model 2 (varying variances, zero covariances), k = 4. The honest part is what each conventional criterion actually contributes to that choice.

What it's for

Run alongside standard reporting, this technique surfaces an at-risk segment that department-, tenure-, and level-based cuts can't isolate — here, the ~19% in the lowest-experience profile, spread evenly across every group. Once that segment can be named, it can be staffed and acted on.
